1 What is a Property VA?

A Property Virtual Assistant (VA) provides remote administrative and operational support to real estate professionals, property managers, and real estate agencies in the Philippines. You help streamline operations, manage listings, coordinate with clients, and handle documentation.

2 Core Responsibilities

  • Managing property listings across multiple platforms
  • Responding to client inquiries via email, phone, and messaging apps
  • Scheduling property viewings and coordinating with agents
  • Preparing documents (contracts, listing agreements, reports)
  • Conducting market research and property valuations
  • Managing social media accounts and marketing campaigns
  • Maintaining CRM databases and client records

üîç How to Research Comparable Properties

Step-by-Step Comparison

  1. Find 3-5 similar properties in same area (within 1-2km radius)
  2. Match property type, size, and age as closely as possible
  3. Check recent sold prices (last 3-6 months)
  4. Look at current listings on Lamudi, Property24, Facebook
  5. Note differences (renovations, view, floor level)
  6. Calculate average price per square meter

‚öñÔ∏è Understanding Philippine Property Laws

Key Legal Principles

  • Foreign Ownership: Foreigners cannot own land, but can own condos (up to 40% foreign ownership per building)
  • Torrens System: PH uses title registration system for land ownership
  • Prescription Period: 10 years for ordinary, 30 years for extraordinary possession
  • Community Property: Property acquired during marriage is conjugal (both spouses own)

üìã Types of Land Titles in PH

Title Hierarchy (Best to Least Secure)

  1. TCT (Transfer Certificate of Title): Most secure, registered with Registry of Deeds, has owner's name
  2. CCT (Condominium Certificate of Title): For condo units, includes unit number and building
  3. OCT (Original Certificate of Title): First title issued for land
  4. Tax Declaration: NOT proof of ownership, only for tax purposes
  5. Deed of Sale: Transfer document, not title itself

Red Flag: If seller only has Tax Declaration or Deed of Sale but no TCT/CCT, verify thoroughly with Registry of Deeds. Could be fake or disputed property.

Verifying Authentic Titles

  • Check with Registry of Deeds in the city/municipality
  • Request Certified True Copy (CTC) of title
  • Verify owner's name matches seller's ID
  • Check for annotations (mortgage, liens, disputes)
  • Look for security features (watermark, seal)
  • Verify TCT number with Registry

üö® Common Property Scams to Avoid

Warning Signs

Fake Titles: Photocopied titles, missing security features, wrong format
Duplicate Selling: Property sold to multiple buyers
Squatter Issues: Professional squatters claim ownership
Boundary Disputes: Neighbors claiming overlap
Estate Sales: Heirs not agreeing, fake heirs appearing
Foreclosed Properties: Bank ownership not clear yet

How to Protect Buyers

  1. Always verify title at Registry of Deeds
  2. Conduct ocular inspection with surveyor
  3. Check property with Barangay for issues
  4. Request tax clearance and receipts
  5. Hire lawyer for title investigation
  6. Insist on meeting at property location
  7. Never pay full amount before title transfer

‚úÖ Seller Verification Checklist

Documents to Request

  • Original TCT/CCT (inspect, don't accept photocopy only)
  • Valid government ID matching title name
  • Latest Tax Declaration and receipts
  • Real Property Tax clearance
  • Lot plan approved by LRA
  • Barangay clearance
  • If married: Marriage certificate + spouse consent
  • If inherited: Death certificate + extrajudicial settlement
  • If corporation: Board resolution, SEC certificate

Red Flags to Watch

  • Seller rushing the transaction ("sign today!")
  • Refusing to meet at the property
  • Price significantly below market (too good to be true)
  • Unwilling to provide original documents
  • No clear explanation for annotations on title
  • Multiple "representatives" claiming to handle sale

üí∞ Taxes & Fees in Property Transactions

Buyer's Responsibilities (Purchase)

  • Transfer Tax: 0.5% - 0.75% of selling price or zonal value (whichever is higher)
  • Documentary Stamp Tax (DST): ‚Ç±15.00 per ‚Ç±1,000 of selling price
  • Registration Fee: Varies, roughly ‚Ç±3,000 - ‚Ç±10,000
  • Notarial Fee: 1% - 2% of selling price
  • Miscellaneous Fees: Certifications, documentary requirements

Seller's Responsibilities (Sale)

  • Capital Gains Tax (CGT): 6% of selling price or zonal value (whichever is higher)
  • OR Income Tax if habitually selling (varies by bracket)
  • Real Property Tax: Must be current (no arrears)
  • Withholding Tax: If applicable, 6% or based on tax bracket

Total Cost Estimate

Buyer typically pays: 3-5% of property value in taxes/fees
Seller typically pays: 6-8% in capital gains tax and fees

Example: ‚Ç±5M property
Buyer: ‚Ç±150,000 - ‚Ç±250,000
Seller: ‚Ç±300,000 - ‚Ç±400,000
